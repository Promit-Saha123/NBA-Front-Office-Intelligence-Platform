# 0003 — Internal Player-Impact Target (Replacement for Next-Season BPM)

**Status:** Accepted — approved by the owner on **2026-07-19**.

## Acceptance Record (2026-07-19)

The owner approved:

1. **Target direction:** replace next-season BPM with an internally computed
   player-contribution metric.
2. **Methodology:** the Option-A-anchored staged hybrid described in this record —
   a regularized team-outcome contribution model first, hardened by the §6
   validation program; Option C reserved for win-conversion calibration and
   cross-checking; Option B standalone rejected.
3. **Working name:** **Player Contribution Estimate (PCE)**.
4. **Initial version name:** **`pce-v1`** (assigned only after the §6.1 validation
   program, including coefficient-stability evaluation, completes; no formula or
   coefficients are frozen by this acceptance).
5. **Next step:** proceed with the search for a legally usable box-score and
   team-outcome source capable of supporting PCE construction.
6. **FiveThirtyEight RAPTOR** remains limited to pipeline prototyping, methodology
   comparison, and historical benchmarking — not a production source.

**Approved epistemic classification:**

* A PCE value is a **statistically estimated metric**.
* Applying frozen, versioned PCE coefficients to canonical player statistics is a
  **deterministic calculation**.
* A next-season PCE forecast is a **model prediction**.
* Rotation minutes generated by the scenario engine are **heuristic assumptions**.
* Team and roster aggregation from fixed projections and minutes is a
  **deterministic calculation**.

Standard descriptive phrase:

> Statistically estimated metric calculated deterministically from fixed,
> versioned coefficients.

PCE must never be described as causal, official, definitive, or equivalent to BPM,
RAPTOR, EPM, or another established metric.

The remainder of this record is the evaluated proposal as approved. Sections below
retain their original proposal wording; where they describe BPM as the then-current
target, that is historical context.

Evidence labels used below: **[verified]** (checked against primary sources or this
repository's audited data), **[inference]** (reasoned but not directly confirmed),
**[unresolved]** (open question requiring research or a decision).

## 1. Context

[Decision 0001](0001-historical-data-source.md) found no legally usable source for
Box Plus/Minus: Sports Reference's terms prohibit ML-training use of their data
without permission [verified — archived terms, 2026-07-18 snapshot], and the owner
has decided not to pursue a paid BPM license for the initial project [verified —
owner instruction, 2026-07-19]. The ML specification permits a target change through
an approved decision record ([ml-specification.md §2](../ml-specification.md))
[verified].

This proposal evaluates replacing next-season BPM with an **internally computed,
reproducible player-contribution metric** whose parameters are *learned from
historical data* — never hand-selected. The replacement target must be:

* reproducible from legally usable data
* consistently computable across historical **and future** seasons
* season-relative where appropriate (standardized within season to absorb era and
  pace drift)
* suitable as the label for next-season prediction
* explainable (decomposable into stat-level contributions)
* versioned (`<metric>-vN`, never silently changed)
* free of hand-selected basketball weights
* usable by the roster scenario engine as its player-impact input
  ([scenario-engine.md §16–§19](../scenario-engine.md))

No final formula is defined or approved in this record.

## 2. Candidate Target Designs

### Option A — Regularized team-outcome contribution model

Learn stat weights by regressing team outcomes on minutes-weighted player
statistics:

```text
player-season statistics (standardized within season)
× minutes share of team
→ aggregated to team-season feature vector
→ regularized regression against team net rating
→ learned stat weights
→ per-player contribution value (weights applied to the player's own stats)
```

* **Unit of observation (weight learning):** team-season. ~30 observations per
  season; a 25–45-season history yields roughly 750–1,350 observations [inference —
  exact count depends on the source selected].
* **Target construction (for the later prediction model):** one row per canonical
  player-season; label = that player's contribution value in season N+1.
* **Identification:** weights are identified from cross-team variation in roster
  statistical profiles versus team net rating. Players are never individually
  identified from team outcomes (that would require lineup-level data); only the
  *value of statistical activities* is estimated, then applied to each player's own
  profile. This is the established Statistical Plus/Minus family of methods
  (Rosenbaum 2004, "Measuring How NBA Players Help Their Teams Win"; BPM itself is
  documented as box-score weights fit against long-run adjusted plus/minus;
  regularized adjusted plus/minus per Sill 2010, MIT Sloan Sports Analytics
  Conference) [verified — published methodologies; their details are public
  documentation, not licensed datasets].
* **Multicollinearity:** box-score rates are strongly correlated (usage with
  scoring volume, ORB% with position). Regularization (ridge/elastic net) is
  mandatory; coefficients must be interpreted as a jointly-estimated bundle, not
  isolated effects [inference].
* **Limited team-season sample:** the observation count constrains feature count;
  a compact feature set (roughly 10–20 standardized rates) is realistic. Feature
  expansion needs explicit justification [inference].
* **Teammate dependence:** box-score production is co-produced (assists require
  finishers; defensive stats depend on scheme). Contribution values partially
  absorb context and role; this must be documented as a known limitation
  [inference — consistent with published critiques of box-score metrics].
* **Shrinkage / regularization:** penalty strength chosen by leave-season-out
  cross-validation, never tuned on the final held-out seasons [inference —
  standard practice; consistent with ml-specification §9/§11].
* **Season normalization:** standardize player rates within season (z-scores) and
  express counting stats per possession or per 36 minutes, so the metric is
  season-relative and era drift is absorbed by construction [inference].
* **Traded players:** weight learning uses player-team stint rows (stint minutes
  share within each team-season); the player's season-level contribution is
  computed from season-aggregate stats. Requires a source with per-team stint rows
  or reconstructable stints [inference].
* **Global vs. season-specific coefficients:** global (pooled) coefficients, with
  coefficient-stability checks across rolling refit windows. Season-specific fits
  (30 observations vs. 10–20 features) would be badly underdetermined [inference].
* **Interpretability:** good — a player's value decomposes exactly into
  (weight × standardized stat) terms, which feeds the explanation-factor system
  directly [inference].
* **Stability:** promoted by pooling and regularization; must be measured, not
  assumed (see §6 Validation) [inference].
* **Causal overstatement risk:** high if mislabeled. The estimate is
  associational: "players whose statistical profiles historically accompany better
  team outcomes score higher." It must never be presented as the causal effect of
  adding the player [inference — mandatory labeling consequence].

### Option B — Transparent box-score contribution index

A standardized composite of box-score and efficiency measures. Manual weights are
prohibited, so weights must be learned or come from an established open
methodology. Evaluation:

* **Ease of computation:** trivial once inputs exist; fully deterministic
  [inference].
* **Temporal consistency:** good with within-season standardization [inference].
* **Interpretability:** high at the arithmetic level, weak at the meaning level —
  without an outcome anchor the index measures *statistical volume/breadth*, not
  contribution to winning [inference].
* **Risk of circularity:** central flaw. If weights are learned, a documented
  outcome is required — and the natural outcome is team performance, which turns
  Option B into Option A. If weights come from an unsupervised method (e.g.
  principal components), the first component is dominated by playing volume and
  validating the index against its own inputs is circular [inference]. Published
  fixed-weight indices either embed hand-chosen constants (prohibited here) or
  were themselves fit against team outcomes (again reducing to Option A)
  [inference].
* **Defensive limitations:** severe — steals and blocks capture a thin slice of
  defense; an index without an outcome anchor cannot even partially compensate
  [inference].
* **Dependence on team context:** same co-production issues as Option A, without
  the outcome anchor that gives the weights meaning [inference].
* **Suitability for roster scenarios:** mechanically usable, but the win-change
  chain (index → team rating → wins) would rest on a metric with no demonstrated
  relationship to team outcomes — the weakest honest claim of the three options
  [inference].

**Assessment:** as a standalone target, Option B either collapses into Option A or
cannot justify its weights. Its legitimate role is as a *descriptive profile*
layer, which the scenario engine already has ([scenario-engine.md §21](../scenario-engine.md)) [verified — spec].

### Option C — Predict team outcomes directly from roster features

Skip the player-level label; aggregate roster features and predict team net rating
or wins.

* **Avoids a player-level target?** Yes for training, but the product requires
  player-level projections: the inference contract, player pages, and scenario
  explanations are all defined per player
  ([ml-specification.md §20](../ml-specification.md),
  [project-specification.md §3](../project-specification.md)) [verified — specs].
* **Usefulness for individual projections:** indirect only — player effects must be
  recovered by counterfactual substitution or attribution methods, which is harder
  to explain and validate than a direct player value [inference].
* **Roster-swap evaluation:** mechanically natural (swap features, re-predict), a
  genuine strength [inference].
* **Sample-size limitations:** ~30 team-seasons per year is very small for a
  flexible model over aggregated roster features; overfitting risk is high
  [inference].
* **Explainability:** weaker — changes attribute to feature aggregates, not to a
  named player's contribution value [inference].
* **Roster and minutes requirements:** every prediction needs a full roster plus
  minutes assumptions, entangling *model prediction* with *heuristic assumption* —
  the epistemic categories the product promises to keep separate
  ([scenario-engine.md §3](../scenario-engine.md)) [verified — spec conflict].
* **Compatibility:** unsuitable as the sole approach; well-suited as the
  **team-rating / win-conversion calibration layer** the scenario engine already
  needs ([scenario-engine.md §19–§20](../scenario-engine.md)) and as a validation
  cross-check for Option A [inference].

## 3. Recommendation — staged hybrid anchored on Option A

1. **First prototype method:** a minimal Option A — pooled ridge regression of
   team-season net rating on minutes-weighted, within-season-standardized player
   rates, with leave-season-out selection of the penalty. Even the prototype uses
   learned weights; no hand-weighted interim index (Option B standalone is
   explicitly not recommended, per the circularity assessment above).
2. **Longer-term validated method:** the same design hardened by the validation
   program in §6 (coefficient stability, era analysis, ablations, uncertainty),
   frozen as version 1 of the metric before the next-season prediction model is
   trained. Option C is reused as the win-conversion calibration and as an
   independent cross-check of whether roster-aggregated contributions track team
   outcomes out of sample.
3. **What the UI can honestly claim:** an internally computed, versioned,
   reproducible *estimate* of a player's statistical contribution, with weights
   learned from historical team outcomes by a documented method; season-relative;
   decomposable into stat-level factors.
4. **What the UI cannot claim:** causal impact of adding/removing the player;
   equivalence or comparability to BPM, RAPTOR, EPM, or any established metric;
   complete capture of defense; validation beyond what §6 has actually completed.

Under the epistemic categories: a *historical* contribution value computed with
frozen coefficients and a pinned data version is a **deterministic calculation with
documented statistical provenance**; a *next-season* projection of it is a **model
prediction**. Whether to surface the first category with a distinct label is an
open question for approval (§9).

## 4. Data Requirements

**Option A (recommended) — required:** stable player ID; stable team ID; season;
team membership with per-team stints for traded players; games played; minutes;
scoring (points, FGA, 3PA, FTA); shooting efficiency (TS% or components); assists;
turnovers; rebounds (ORB/DRB or rates); steals; blocks; team possessions (or pace);
team offensive/defensive/net rating (or points for/against + possessions to derive
them).
**Desirable:** games started; usage rate; rate versions of all counting stats;
position; age; team wins (for win-conversion calibration); playoff/regular-season
separation.
**Unavailable but useful:** lineup/stint-level plus-minus (would allow RAPM-style
validation); tracking data; opponent-adjusted schedules [unresolved — no permitted
source identified].

**Option B — required:** the player-stat subset above (no team outcomes).
**Option C — required:** everything in Option A *plus* full roster composition and
actual minutes distributions per team-season; team wins.

**Critical gap:** the pinned FiveThirtyEight RAPTOR snapshot contains **none of the
box-score fields** (only minutes, possessions, and RAPTOR-family values)
[verified — audit], so no option is computable from data currently on hand.

## 5. Data-Source Implications

Changing the target away from BPM removes the proprietary *label* problem — the
metric would be computed by us, from raw statistics, under our own versioned
methodology. It does **not** solve access to the raw statistics themselves: every
option still requires licensed player box-score and team-outcome data, historical
and current, and decision 0001's findings stand (NBA.com terms restrict statistical
databases without consent; Sports Reference data is unavailable for ML use)
[verified — 0001 evidence].

The next data-source search is therefore defined by:

* legally usable current **and** historical box-score data
* clear provenance and chain of redistribution
* redistribution rights compatible with a public-facing product
* stable player and team identifiers
* enough consecutive seasons for weight learning plus rolling backtests
* coverage of the latest completed season
* team-level outcomes (ratings or derivable points/possessions)
* credible continued future availability

**FiveThirtyEight RAPTOR is not approved as the production source** and remains
limited to: pipeline prototyping, methodology comparison, and historical
benchmarking (e.g., correlating a candidate contribution metric against historical
RAPTOR under CC BY 4.0, which is permitted) [verified — license; scope per 0001].

## 6. Validation Design

Three separate evaluation problems, which must never be conflated:

**(1) Validating the constructed metric itself** (before any prediction model):

* *Stability across seasons:* rank correlation of player values season-to-season
  for continuing players; sensitivity of values to one-season-in/out refits.
* *Relationship with future team performance:* do minutes-weighted roster sums in
  season N correlate with team net rating in season N (in-sample reconstruction,
  leave-season-out) and season N+1 (the property the product actually uses)?
* *Comparison against permitted references:* correlation and disagreement analysis
  vs. historical RAPTOR (CC BY 4.0 — permitted benchmark). BPM comparison is
  **not** performed — no permitted access [verified — 0001].
* *Sensitivity to minutes:* value distribution vs. minutes tiers; behavior at
  low-minute extremes; minimum-minutes threshold analysis per ml-specification §15.
* *Traded players:* agreement between stint-weighted and season-aggregate values.
* *By position and role:* value distributions by position and starter/reserve role
  to detect systematic role bias (box-score methods typically undervalue
  low-usage defensive roles [inference]).
* *Outlier analysis:* inspect extreme values for artifacts (tiny samples, unusual
  stat profiles).
* *Coefficient stability:* coefficients across rolling training windows and eras;
  sign flips or large swings are red flags.
* *Ablation tests:* remove stat groups (e.g., defensive stats, efficiency) and
  measure reconstruction loss — documents what information the metric actually
  uses.
* *Uncertainty:* bootstrap (resampling team-seasons) for coefficient and
  player-value intervals.
* *Time-based validation throughout:* all tuning by leave-season-out or rolling
  windows; a final untouched season block for confirmation.

**(2) Predicting the metric for next season:** exactly the existing
ml-specification program (baselines §10, rolling backtests §9, metrics §12) with
the new target substituted — unchanged in structure [verified — spec].

**(3) Using projected contributions in roster scenarios:** governed by the
scenario-engine spec (aggregation §17, team-rating conversion §19, win conversion
§20) and validated separately; nothing in this proposal validates win conversion
[verified — spec boundary].

## 7. Naming

Proposed working name: **Player Contribution Estimate (PCE)**, versioned as
`pce-v1`, `pce-v2`, … with the version always displayed alongside the value.

Rationale: "Estimate" is in the name; no causal, official, or definitive
connotation; no similarity to BPM, RAPTOR, or EPM that could imply equivalence.
Alternatives considered: "Estimated Player Contribution" (acronym EPC sits
uncomfortably close to EPM), "Regularized Player Contribution" (leaks methodology
into a user-facing name). Final name choice requires owner approval (§9).
